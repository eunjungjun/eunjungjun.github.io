Youzi: Generative AI For Social Mandarin Immersion

Reminiscing about designing a generative AI and social Mandarin learning app

—Overview— What is Youzi


Youzi is a Mandarin language learning app, born from the discovery that there is an unmet need in the American-born Chinese (i.e. ABC) population. Many ABCs regret not focusing more on learning Mandarin earlier in their lives. As young adults, they often feel embarrassed when trying to speak Mandarin. Our team sought to answer the following problem: how could we help young ABCs overcome their anxiety in speaking Mandarin while also helping them to build and maintain relationships within the Mandarin speaking community?

—Taking the first step— Understanding our users


User interviews


Our team discovered this neglected market through conducting user interviews during the fall of 2023. After officially beginning this capstone project at the start of 2024, we pursued further research through in-depth user interviews with 3 students at UC Berkeley, who were taking Mandarin classes at the time. In these interviews, we determined the following user needs that we had to account for in our design solutions:

  1. ABCs want more contextual, relevant learning, rather than learning vocabulary words in isolation
  2. Other solutions lacked personalization or skill-matching

Additionally, we had to make sure that our design fit the constraint of being a mobile solution, as we found that contextual learning was most effective in small and frequent opportunities throughout the day. This meant that the smartphone was the ideal vehicle in delivering our solutions.



Affinity mapping


Our team's next step was to create an affinity map from the insights that we gained from the interviews. We divided the insights into 3 main categories of design directions that we needed to implement within the app:

  1. Conversations: reduce barrier to starting conversations in Mandarin
  2. Engagement: keep users interested in continuing to use the app
  3. Tools: create features to help users practice contextual learning



—Starting the design process— Design, critique, repeat


Brainstorming & sketches


First, our team held a brainstorming session, where we each shared potential design solutions that we could implement within the app. Although we considered many ideas, we ultimately decided that the following features seemed the most promising:

  1. Interest selector: curate prompts based on user's overall likes
  2. Vibes: let user pick the mood and type of conversation prompts they want
  3. Vocabulary list: provide words that are relevant to prompt and user's level
  4. Recordings: allow user to record responses for later review
  5. Transcripts: create recording transcripts to help users practice reading



User flow


Using the results from our brainstorming, we then planned out the overall user flow and structure of the app, which would be divided into 4 main parts:

  1. Sign up: create a unique profile to keep track of progress
  2. Onboarding: view a series of tutorials to become familiar with the app
  3. Conversation mode: get personalized prompts to spark fun conversations
  4. Review mode: revisit vocabulary and past prompts to retain learnings

In conversation mode, users would receive daily curated prompts, based on their interests, that would help them more easily start conversations in Mandarin with their speaking partners.

Review mode is a subflow meant to help users retain what they had learned during their conversations. Within this mode, the users can access an archive of vocabulary and completed prompts.



Wireframing & low-fi prototype


In this stage, we translated the most promising ideas from our rough sketches into low-fidelity wireframes in Figma. The wireframes were created in greyscale in order to focus on the flow and information architecture of the app.

We built upon the ideas we had brainstormed in the previous step--for the landing page specifically, we played around with various aspects, like where to place the filters, what colors worked best, what shapes to use, etc.



Mid-fi prototype


After a round of critique from our client, users, and colleagues, we created a higher fidelity prototype in Figma. In this version, our group decided that the landing page should:

  1. Have the filters on the left side of the page since we thought this would generate the most user familiarity
  2. Additionally, the filters section as a whole should be expandable and collapsible due to the high number of tags that Ersilia utilizes



—The final design— Reflecting on the hi-fi prototype & the process


❮ ❯

Design solutions


In the hi-fi prototype, we settled on the below design solutions that would best meet the design principles that we decided upon at the beginning of the project.

Landing page:

  1. Color-coded filters and sorting system to find relevant models
  2. Model previews displayed as standardized cards with short summaries
  3. If no relevant models, users could see closest matches or contact Ersilia

Data modal:

  1. Defaulted to using model online, emphasizing the shift from CLI to GUI
  2. 2 tab format allowed users to switch to the CLI in case of Internet issues
  3. Users were given relevant information without being overwhelmed

Model simulation:

  1. Ability to input multiple compounds to make model utilization easier
  2. Progress bar added so users know how long it will take for models to run
  3. Option to copy outputs and feed this info as input into another model

Model hub guide:

  1. Approachable, easy to understand 3 step guide
  2. Available from landing page so users know about this resource
  3. Expand and collapse all button added for easy viewing



Conclusion & reflections


Our client was thrilled with the high-fidelity designs that we delivered and expressed that they wanted to implement our design in the future once they had more resources to dedicate to front-end development. Some of the final thoughts I had when completing the project were:

Designing for an unfamiliar field comes with many challenges: While some of my teammates had backgrounds in biology, all of us had to rapidly become comfortable with Ersilia's technical jargon and with exploring unknown territory.

Communication is essential in a team: This was my first time working closely with other designers. Open communication and trust were key in making sure that our design processes aligned in order to create a cohesive prototype.

What I would do with more time: If we had more time, I would have liked to explore the communicative aspect of the model hub and create other ways for users to interact with Ersilia's team since our client valued their bond with users.
