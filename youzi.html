Youzi: Generative AI For Social Mandarin Immersion

Reminiscing about designing a generative AI and social Mandarin learning app

—Overview— What is Youzi


Youzi is a Mandarin language learning app, born from the discovery that there is an unmet need in the American-born Chinese (i.e. ABC) population. Many ABCs regret not focusing more on learning Mandarin earlier in their lives. As young adults, they often feel embarrassed when trying to speak Mandarin. Our team sought to answer the following problem: how could we help young ABCs overcome their anxiety in speaking Mandarin while also helping them to build and maintain relationships within the Mandarin speaking community?

—Taking the first step— Understanding our users


User interviews


Our team discovered this neglected market through conducting user interviews during the fall of 2023. After officially beginning this capstone project at the start of 2024, I pursued further research through in-depth user interviews with 3 UC Berkeley students, who were taking Mandarin classes at the time. In these interviews, I determined the following user needs:

  1. ABCs wanted more contextual, relevant learning, rather than learning vocabulary words in isolation
  2. Other solutions lacked personalization or skill-matching

Additionally, one constraint was that the design needed to be mobile, as I found that contextual learning was most effective in small, frequent opportunities, making the smartphone the ideal vehicle in delivering my design solutions.



Affinity mapping


My next step was to create an affinity map from the insights that I gained from the interviews. I divided the insights into 3 main categories of design directions that I needed to implement within the app:

  1. Conversations: reduce barrier to starting conversations in Mandarin
  2. Engagement: keep users interested in continuing to use the app
  3. Tools: create features to help users practice contextual learning



—Starting the design process— Design, critique, repeat


Brainstorming & sketches


After sharing my findings with the team, we held a brainstorming session, where we determined that the best way to address our users' needs was to use generative AI to create interesting, personalized conversational prompts that would help ABCs ease themselves into using Mandarin more often and create lasting memories with their speaking partners.

We then sketched and shared potential design solutions, and after considering many ideas, we decided that the following features seemed the most promising:

  1. Interest selection: curate prompts based on user's overall likes
  2. Vibes: let user pick the mood and type of conversation prompts they want
  3. Vocabulary list: provide words that are relevant to prompt and user's level
  4. Recordings: allow user to record responses for later review
  5. Transcripts: create recording transcripts to help users practice reading



User flow


Using the results from our brainstorming, I then planned out the overall user flow and structure of the app, which would be divided into 4 main parts:

  1. Sign up: create a unique profile to keep track of progress
  2. Onboarding: view a series of tutorials to become familiar with the app
  3. Conversation mode: get personalized prompts to spark fun conversations
  4. Review mode: revisit vocabulary and past prompts to retain learnings



Low-fi wireframes & mid-fi prototype


In this stage, I first translated the rough sketches and user flow diagram into low-fidelity wireframes in Figma. I decided to create the wireframes in greyscale in order to focus on the flow and information architecture of the app.

For the mid-fidelity prototype, I began to play around with colors and fonts that I thought would fit the adjectives that I wanted the app's design to embody: playful, bubbly, and welcoming.

Initially, I explored using pink and blue accents but ultimately decided upon using a warm orange and green color scheme instead to better fit the name of our app, as Youzi means "pomelo" in Mandarin.



Usability testing & hi-fi prototype


While creating the high-fidelity prototypes, I continued to gather feedback by conducting 5 usability tests with young adult ABCs and our team's advisors. Through these usability sessions, I identified opportunities for improvements.

First, the information architecture of the prompt screens needed to be reworked. Generally, ABCs were more confident in their listening skills, but the audio button was hard to see. Also, the English translation was displayed before the Mandarin prompt, discouraging ABCs from practicing reading.

Next, I discovered that the vibes were not granular enough. The users expressed that there were topics they would never discuss with certain speaking partners. For example, 1 user said that while she would be open to discussing gossip prompts with her parents, she would not want them to be romance-related.

Finally, our advisor provided feedback that while the app's design was aesthetically pleasing, it needed further forms of user engagement.

To address these concerns, I made the following changes within my design:

  1. Information architecture:
    • Audio button moved to left of prompt to be more visible
    • Switched order of prompts to display Mandarin version first
    • Prompt collapsed by default to encourage reading skills
  2. Vibes:
    • Combined similar vibes into 4 more distinct categories
    • Then, divided the 4 main vibes into 4 smaller sub-vibes each
  3. Engagement:
    • Added option of playing a related YouTube video for each prompt
    • Created Xiao-you, an interactive, expressive character



—Final design & MVP— The fruits of our labor


❮ ❯

Design solutions


Sign up & onboarding:

  1. Interest selection to provide personalized experience, based on user's likes
  2. Short, straightforward tutorials to ease user into the app
  3. Simple hub screen with two choices for returning users

Conversation mode:

  1. Daily, granular vibe selection to curate what kind of prompts user saw
  2. Expressive Xiao-you, a character to gently engage and teach user
  3. Dictionary modal to copy, learn, hear, or save vocabulary words

Review mode:

  1. Customized vocabulary list with users' saved words and app's suggestions
  2. Archive with option to play conversation recordings and edit transcripts
  3. Option to press word in transcript to view pinyin and practice reading



Design system


In order to streamline my work, I created a design system, composed of 2 parts:

  1. Style guide: outline of font and color scheme used in app
  2. Component library: Figma components used to create consistent designs



Conclusion & reflections


Our team presented the Youzi app as our UC Berkeley MIMS (Master of Information Management & Systems) capstone project in front of an audience of over 100 people. We received very positive feedback on the project from our colleagues, UC Berkeley faculty members, and project advisors. Closely working with my teammates on Youzi taught me that:

Balancing a project's scope with its timeline is incredibly difficult: Although there were many features that we wanted to include, we realized that we had to be selective and realistic about what features would fit into the short timeline.

It is okay to discard ideas that do not fit the project: There were some ideas that we wanted to include, such as online community features, but we learned that these ideas did not necessarily solve the user problem that we defined.

What I would do with more time: Our team is currently planning to continue work on Youzi in the near future--we will mainly be focusing on building our business model and securing additional funding for the project.
