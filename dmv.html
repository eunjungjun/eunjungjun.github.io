Reimagining Oregon's DMV

A tale of diving into the redesign of the Oregon DMV Real ID process

—Overview— Framing the problem


Starting from 2025, Real IDs are necessary in order to board domestic flights. However, creating appointments for the DMV can be frustrating, time-consuming, and tedious. For this project, the design challenge I faced was: How can I redesign the Oregon DMV Real ID process to help more users complete the application flow as well as reduce business costs for the DMV (e.g. fewer help desk calls)?

Role

Solo UX designer

My work

· Figma prototyping
· Usability tests
· Sketches

Timeline

4 weeks
(Sep - Oct 2022)



—Conducting research— Observing usability issues


Usability tests


Before I began my design process, I decided to conduct 2 in-person usability tests to directly observe how users unfamiliar with the Oregon DMV's Real ID process would interact with the website. From these tests, I observed the following:

  1. Users felt overwhelmed by how information on the page was presented
  2. Too many steps included redundant information that was not relevant to the users
  3. The overall design felt inconsistent from page to page and defied user expectations



—Beginning the redesign— Going through the initial iterations


Sketches


Taking what I learned from the usability tests, I sketched out a potential Real ID flow, considering different alternatives for the home page, the actual scheduling process, and the map/calendar view.

Critique from my colleagues helped me decide what to keep and what to change in my next iteration:
  1. The 6-icon menu was the most straightforward of the home page options
  2. A progress bar made it easy to know exactly how many steps were left in the process
  3. While the color-coded scale for the calendar was an interesting idea, it wasn't very intuitive



Wireframing & low-fi prototype


For my next iteration, I created a wireframe/low-fi prototype that fleshed out the ideas that were received positively from the previous critique. However, another round of critique alerted me to the following issues:

  1. Although I reduced the amount of overall copy, some of the wording was confusing or vague
  2. The pop-up informational modal was unexpected and distracting because it filled up most of the screen
  3. The calendar legend felt unnecessary and redundant
  4. Since the goal is to help the user complete the application process, there is no need for a "Start Over" button
  5. Some elements were too small and not very accessible



—My completed redesign— What I learned & the hi-fi prototype


Conclusion & reflections


Copy is incredibly important to a good design: One of my biggest challenges for this project was cutting the amount of copy down while also still communicating a message effectively.

Carefully consider what to prioritize: Throughout the design process, I had to think deeply about what features or info from the original design were most important to include since I had very limited screen real estate to work with.

What I would do with more time: I would be interested in conducting more research, such as additional usability tests, to verify what parts of my design are effective and what parts need to be refined further.



Design solutions


Users are shown their progress and only relevant info: To encourage users to complete the flow, a progress bar was added to show how many steps remained. In addition, redundant/irrelevant copy was minimized as much as possible.

Each step seeks to preemptively help users: At the bottom of each page, there is an expandable section that includes answers to common questions or links to resources for users in order to reduce overall help desk calls to the DMV.

Users are given feedback every step of the way: On each screen, toast messages appear to either inform the user that they successfully completed a step or to let them know about an error state that needs to be resolved.
