Ersilia: Making Access To Machine Learning Models Easy

The story of transforming a CLI-based machine-learning model hub into a GUI

—Overview— What is Ersilia


Ersilia is a nonprofit organization based in Spain. Its mission is to develop and distribute machine learning models to aid infectious disease research. At the start of the project, Ersilia's model hub utilized a command-line interface (CLI); however, Ersilia's users were having trouble understanding how to utilize the platform's features. My team and I aimed to solve the following design problem: how could we redesign the CLI into an accessible graphical user interface (GUI) for Ersilia's users, who are scientists with resource constraints?

—Our initial research— Framing the problem


User & stakeholder interviews


Our team first conducted 8 user and stakeholder interviews, where we spoke to our client as well as the users and developers of Ersilia's model hub. Through these interviews, we determined the following constraints:

  1. Many of the research scientists who used the model hub were less technically savvy
  2. Scientists had limited Internet access and connectivity since they were located in developing countries
  3. The GUI needed to be browser-based for easy access



Competitor analysis


Then, our team performed an in-depth competitor analysis into how other model hubs were designed, and we pinpointed their strengths and weaknesses.

After doing so, we came up with the following design principles that we wanted to emphasize in our design to make us stand out from similar platforms:

  1. Simplicity: reduce barrier to adoption with a clean front-end design
  2. Homogeneity: standardize how models appear in the interface
  3. Discoverable models: help users find relevant models easily
  4. Author credit: give visibility to all original model authors
  5. Documentation: build trust with users using sufficient documentation
  6. Scalability: design should scale as number of models grow
  7. Sense of purpose: users should feel connected with Ersilia's mission
  8. Proximity to the team: users should feel welcome to provide feedback



—Gathering inspiration for designs— Our first iterations


Brainstorming & sketches


We first started with a brainstorming session, where we sketched out potential design ideas together. In this step, we agreed that we needed to accomplish the following in our design while still following the design principles above:

  1. Create an easy way for users to sort and filter for relevant models
  2. Present information, like model results, in an easily digestible manner
  3. Provide users resources to help them adjust to Ersilia's learning curve



Low-fi wireframes


From this step on, our group split the low-fidelity design into equal parts because of the short project timeframe, and we each took responsibility for 1 part. However, we made sure to keep our designs consistent with each other by providing and receiving feedback as a group throughout the design process. I was in charge of designing the landing page of the model hub.

We built upon the ideas we had brainstormed in the previous step--for the landing page specifically, we played around with various aspects, like where to place the filters, what colors worked best, what shapes to use, etc.



Mid-fi prototype


After a round of critique from our client, users, and colleagues, we created a higher fidelity prototype in Figma. In this version, our group decided that the landing page should:

  1. Have the filters on the left side of the page since we thought this would generate the most user familiarity
  2. Have the filters section as a whole be expandable and collapsible due to the high number of tags that Ersilia utilizes



—The final design— Reflecting on the hi-fi prototype & the process


❮ ❯

Design solutions


Landing page:

  1. Color-coded filters and sorting system to find relevant models
  2. Model previews displayed as standardized cards with short summaries
  3. If no relevant models, users could see closest matches or contact Ersilia

Data modal:

  1. Defaulted to using model online, emphasizing the shift from CLI to GUI
  2. 2 tab format allowed users to switch to the CLI in case of Internet issues
  3. Users were given relevant information in a compact format

Model simulation:

  1. Ability to input multiple compounds to make model utilization easier
  2. Progress bar added so users knew how long it will take for models to run
  3. Option to copy outputs and feed this info as input into another model

Model hub guide:

  1. Approachable, easy to understand 3 step guide
  2. Available from landing page so users knew about this resource
  3. Expand and collapse all button added for easy viewing



Conclusion & reflections


Our client was thrilled with the high-fidelity designs that we delivered and expressed that they wanted to implement our design in the future once they had more resources to dedicate to front-end development. Some of the final thoughts I had when completing the project were:

Designing for an unfamiliar field comes with many challenges: While some of my teammates had backgrounds in biology, all of us had to rapidly become comfortable with Ersilia's technical jargon and with exploring unknown territory.

Communication is essential in a team: This was my first time working closely with other designers. Open communication and trust were key in making sure that our design processes aligned in order to create a cohesive prototype.

What I would do with more time: If we had more time, I would have liked to explore the communicative aspect of the model hub and create other ways for users to interact with Ersilia's team since our client valued their bond with users.
