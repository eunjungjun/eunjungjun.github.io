Jumbo: Redesigning A Website From The Ground Up

A tale of the redesign of Jumbo Privacy's FAQ website and improving metrics

—Overview— What is Jumbo


Jumbo Privacy is an app startup that aims to help users manage their online presence and protect their data privacy. The scope of this project was ambiguous because my only assigned task was to make the overall user experience of the FAQ better. The nebulous guidelines made it difficult for me to initially decide where to start. However, observation of the user experience from 2 different perspectives, from interactions through tickets and from user interviews, helped inform what design decision paths I needed to pursue.

—The starting line— The journey into the jungle that is the user


Support team ↔ user considerations


Jumbo's support team handled hundreds of user tickets every week. Its main form of communication was through email, and the team often provided links to resources for users. Thus, it was important that my redesign would make it easier for the support team to direct users to helpful, relevant articles.

In addition, I also had to consider the following constraints:

  1. Jumbo's FAQ was hosted on Help Scout, so I could not change the overall site architecture
  2. A large portion of Jumbo's userbase was elderly and less technically savvy, so the site needed to be accessible and easy to use



Usability testing


At the start of my research, I conducted 5 usability tests and watched users freely interact with the FAQ. By doing so, I identified the following pain points:

  1. Navigation was time-consuming because users had to constantly go back and forth between pages to read multiple articles
  2. The tutorials were hard to follow because users had to pinch and zoom to see any associated images and then zoom back out to read the steps



Journey map interviews


Then, through 20 journey mapping interviews, I discovered that many of our users followed similar journeys in getting to Jumbo:

  1. Trigger: experienced a significant data concern, like a password breach
  2. Search: looked for a solution through various sources, like the App Store
  3. Validation: after finding Jumbo, read reviews or watched videos about it
  4. Adoption: began using the app on the go and in short bursts
  5. Contact: reached out to the support team for any further problems



—Diving deeper— Defining and coding


Problem statement


After conducting extensive research, I was able to narrow down the scope of the project and define a specific problem statement to guide my designs: how could I redesign the FAQ website so that it is more accessible to Jumbo's userbase while also assisting the support team?

With this, I was ready to begin the process of redesigning and recoding the website's front-end, using a mix of HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. During this period, I regularly sought out feedback, both internally from my teammates as well as externally from Jumbo's users, to see what changes worked best.



—Final design & impact— The changes born from my research


❮ ❯

Design solutions


After many iterations, I created design solutions that followed the constraints set earlier in the project while also addressing the problem statement that I defined after much research.

Single page navigation:

  1. Related articles could be viewed on 1 page without changing pages
  2. Articles could be expanded or collapsed with a single tap

Lightbox-based tutorials:

  1. All images could be enlarged for easier viewing
  2. Tutorial text could be read at same time, minimizing back-and-forth
  3. Users could jump to any point in tutorial by using image previews

Direct article links:

  1. Each FAQ article was assigned its own unique ID
  2. By adjusting URL ID, support team can send users directly to any article
  3. JavaScript code automatically expands the article to reduce friction



Conclusion & reflections


The impact of my design decisions were significant--average ticket volume for the support team went down by over 50% over the course of several months. In addition, average daily visits to the FAQ went up, and qualitative feedback from follow-up user interviews were incredibly positive. Finally, I came away from this experience with several learnings:

Ambiguity is not always a bad thing: It gave me the freedom to delve further into defining the problem statement myself and to have confidence in my resulting design decisions.

Good UX is not just about the end users: It should also take into account those in the company who will be frequently interacting with end users as any support team is a valuable customer touchpoint.

What I would do with more time: If I had more time for this project, I would have liked to continue exploring other ways to help reduce the workload for the support team further.
